What a Marriage Biodata Really Does

People call it a "resume for marriage." That's a fair starting point, but it misses something important.

A job resume proves you're qualified for a role. A biodata for marriage lets two families understand each other — your values, your faith, your upbringing, your profession, and the kind of future you want to build.

By the time families sit down to discuss a match, your biodata has already made the first impression. A strong bio data format for marriage introduces who you are, represents your family well, and shows you're taking this seriously — all in one or two clean pages.

Format Guide

What Every Marriage Biodata Should Include

There's no single official standard, but every effective biodata for marriage covers the same core ground.

01

Personal Details — Your Opening Statement

Your full name, date of birth, place of birth, height, and current city go here. Date of birth is one of the most important attributes — families use it for age checks and, in Hindu communities, for Kundali (horoscope) matching. Keep everything accurate. Families cross-check early.

02

Educational & Professional — Your Stability Story

This is where families quietly ask: Is this person settled? Be specific. "Working in IT" tells a family nothing. "Senior Cloud Engineer at TCS, Bengaluru — B.Tech from NIT Surathkal, 2019" tells them your field, employer, and level in one line. For NRI candidates, add country, visa category, and plans.

03

Family Details — The Section Parents Read Twice

In South Asian matchmaking, families marry into families. Include parents' names and occupations, number of siblings and whether they're married, and family type — nuclear or joint. A short, warm line about your family's character gives the other side a real picture.

04

Astrological & Cultural — For Communities That Need It

For Hindu families, include Rashi, Nakshatra, Gotra, time of birth for Kundali, and Manglik status. For Muslim biodata — community, sect, and faith background. For Sikh biodata, Gotra and family background take priority. All fields can be added or removed on our platform in seconds.

05

Hobbies & Expectations — Where Most Biodata Fall Flat

This is the most overlooked section — and the easiest place to stand out. "I enjoy travelling, reading, and family time" says nothing. "I've been learning Bharatanatyam for eleven years" is a real detail. Write it the way you'd talk — not like you're filling out a form.

06

Contact Details — Don't Make Families Search

Include a family member's name, phone number, and email. Double-check the number before your biodata starts moving through networks. Wrong contact details are one of the most common problems in biodata already circulating.

Key Differences

Marriage Biodata for Girl vs Boy

👩 For Her

Biodata for Marriage for Girl

A marriage biodata for girl gets looked at closely for values, character, and family background alongside career credentials. Working women should list their job title and employer with full confidence — it belongs front and centre.

Two things families want clear answers on: whether you plan to keep working after marriage, and whether you're open to relocating. Say both honestly — ambiguity creates more delays than a direct answer ever would.

For your photo, use a clear, naturally lit image where your face is fully visible. Skip filtered or cropped social media shots.

👨 For Him

Marriage Biodata for Boy

A marriage biodata for boy puts more weight on career stability and future prospects. Families want to know where you work, what you do, and how settled your path is. Be specific about your role and employer — not just your industry.

For NRI boys, include visa status, country and city, and your long-term plans. Families looking at an international match need this upfront.
